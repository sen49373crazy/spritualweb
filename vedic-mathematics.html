Aryabhata

476 - 550 CE

The father of Indian mathematics and astronomy, Aryabhata revolutionized mathematical thinking with his groundbreaking work "Aryabhatiya". He introduced the concept of zero as a number and developed the place-value system that forms the foundation of modern mathematics.

Brahmagupta

598 - 668 CE

A mathematical genius who formalized the rules of arithmetic with zero and negative numbers. His work "Brahmasphutasiddhanta" laid the foundation for algebraic thinking and established rules that are still taught in schools today.

Key Contributions:

  • Rules for arithmetic with zero and negative numbers
  • Brahmagupta's formula for cyclic quadrilaterals
  • Solutions to quadratic equations
  • Pell's equation (centuries before Pell)
  • Interpolation formulas

Bhaskara II (Bhaskaracharya)

1114 - 1185 CE

Known as the greatest mathematician of medieval India, Bhaskara II wrote the famous "Lilavati" and "Bijaganita", which covered arithmetic, algebra, and astronomy. His work on calculus predated Newton and Leibniz by centuries.

Key Contributions:

  • Early concepts of differential calculus
  • Solutions to Diophantine equations
  • Chakravala method for solving equations

1340 - 1425 CE

The founder of the Kerala School of Mathematics, Madhava discovered infinite series expansions for trigonometric functions, laying the groundwork for calculus 300 years before it was developed in Europe.

Panini

~500 - 400 BCE

Though primarily known as a grammarian, Panini's "Ashtadhyayi" employed sophisticated mathematical concepts including recursion, transformation rules, and meta-rules that influenced formal language theory and computer science.

Key Contributions:

  • Formal grammar system with mathematical precision
  • Recursive rules and meta-rules
  • Algebraic notation for linguistic structures
  • Influenced modern computer science
  • Backus-Naur form precursor
